The red (negative) bars on the put vanna chart represent puts that dealers are short — meaning customers bought those puts from dealers. Volland charts show the dealer's exposure, not the customer's. So negative (red) vanna exposure on puts means dealers sold those puts and are now carrying the hedging obligation. On the right chart (call vanna), that big green bar around 7000 means dealers are long calls at that strike. Customers sold those calls to dealers. Since dealers own them, the vanna exposure is positive and stabilizing — as spot rises toward 7000 with falling vol, the delta on those calls decays and dealers' hedging flows act as a natural dampener rather than an accelerant. So to summarize: * Put Vanna chart (left): Red = dealers short puts (customers bought them). Green = dealers long puts (customers sold them). * Call Vanna chart (right): Green = dealers long calls (customers sold them). Red = dealers short calls (customers bought them). * That green bar at 7000 on the call side is actually a nice magnet/stabilizer if we were to rally toward it — dealers long those calls would be selling into strength as delta increases, capping upside momentum near that level. Separately, if price does come down into 6850 you have dealer support.
